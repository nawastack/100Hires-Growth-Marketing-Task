AI search usage is up 70% year over year. Trust in it dropped 28 points in the same period. New Fractl and [Search Engine Land](https://www.linkedin.com/company/search-engine-land?trk=public%5Fprofile%5F%5Fposts-text) research surveyed over 1,000 consumers and 150 marketers. The findings are a wake-up call for anyone scaling content with AI. A few numbers worth sitting with: 39% of consumers now say heavy AI use in a brand's marketing reduces their trust, up from 20% last year. Over 80% want AI-generated content clearly labeled, across every format. Only 20% of brands actually disclose it. On the marketing side: AI now touches 53% of marketing work, up from 38% last year. But only 26% say AI made their work faster and better. Nearly half say it made their work faster but more generic. The most important line in the whole report: original research and proprietary data is the strategy that builds the strongest moat against AI commoditization, yet it is the least prioritized tactic at just 15%. Translation: everyone is racing to scale AI content. Almost nobody is investing in the thing AI cannot replicate. That gap is where the real opportunity sits right now. Bing just gave website owners a way to see exactly how AI is citing their content. Four new features rolling out in Bing Webmaster Tools: Intents, Topics, Citation Share, and Compare. Intents shows you the broader purpose behind the queries citing your content - informational, commercial, research, learning, and more. Topics groups related queries into themes, the way AI systems actually think rather than isolated keywords. The most useful one: Citation Share. It shows what percentage of citations your site gets for a specific query compared to every other cited source. Not a ranking system, just a clear view of how much visibility you actually have. Compare lets you overlay past periods to track how that visibility shifts over time. Bing beat Google to this - their AI performance report launched in February, months before Google's Search Console version. Still no click data from either platform yet. But this is the clearest signal yet that AI visibility reporting is becoming a real discipline, not an afterthought. 87% of creators say AI has accelerated the growth of their business or audience. But 85% still say the final creative decision should always be theirs.[Adobe](https://www.linkedin.com/company/adobe?trk=public%5Fprofile%5F%5Fposts-text) just surveyed 16,000+ creators across 8 countries for its 2026 Creators' Toolkit Report. The findings line up exactly with what I keep saying about AI and content. 75% call creative AI integrated or essential to their workflow. 93% say it helps them produce content faster. But 57% say AI outputs still need moderate to extensive editing before they are ready to publish. Faster-to-draft is not the same as ready-to-publish. That gap is where the actual work still happens. The most interesting finding: as more people use AI, content volume goes up but standing out gets harder. 53% blame sheer content volume. 42% say AI-generated content is making unique voices harder to find. The creators winning anyway are not the ones avoiding AI. They are the ones who figured out how to make AI sound like them. 85% say their AI-assisted work still reflects their unique voice. Speed is now table stakes. Voice is the differentiator. I use both daily. Here is when I reach for each one and why it matters which you pick for which task. 𝗖𝗹𝗮𝘂𝗱𝗲 - 𝗠𝘆 𝗱𝗲𝗳𝗮𝘂𝗹𝘁 𝗳𝗼𝗿 𝗮𝗻𝘆𝘁𝗵𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝘁𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝗿𝗲𝗾𝘂𝗶𝗿𝗲𝘀 𝗻𝘂𝗮𝗻𝗰𝗲. Strategy documents. Long-form thinking. Editing my own writing without flattening my voice. Working through a messaging problem where I need to reason through multiple angles before landing on an answer. Claude tends to hold context better across longer conversations and pushes back more when my thinking has a gap in it. 𝗖𝗵𝗮𝘁𝗚𝗣𝗧 - 𝗠𝘆 𝗱𝗲𝗳𝗮𝘂𝗹𝘁 𝗳𝗼𝗿 𝗳𝗮𝘀𝘁, 𝗽𝘂𝗻𝗰𝗵𝘆 𝗼𝘂𝘁𝗽𝘂𝘁. Subject line variations. Quick outreach copy. Brainstorming a list of angles fast. When I need volume and speed more than depth, ChatGPT gets there quicker. 𝗪𝗵𝗲𝗿𝗲 𝗜 𝘀𝗲𝗲 𝗽𝗲𝗼𝗽𝗹𝗲 𝗴𝗲𝘁 𝘁𝗵𝗶𝘀 𝘄𝗿𝗼𝗻𝗴 Using one tool for everything. Treating AI like a single utility instead of recognising that different models have different strengths. The founders getting the most value from AI right now are the ones who know which tool to reach for based on the task, not the ones loyal to a single platform. 𝗠𝘆 𝗿𝘂𝗹𝗲 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝘂𝗺𝗯: If the task requires judgment and nuance, Claude. If the task requires speed and volume, ChatGPT. From being laid off… To having brands pay her at LEAST $5,000 for a single Instagram reel. Most creators stay stuck charging peanuts, saying yes to everything, and burning out as the face of their brand. On the second episode of How We Profit, I sat down with Tara LaFerrara, founder of Broads, to break down how she turned a $99 program into a seven-figure fitness brand. 🎧 Watch the FULL episode on YouTube: [https://lnkd.in/gn-GmUCF](https://lnkd.in/gn-GmUCF?trk=public%5Fprofile%5F%5Freactions-text) If you're building a creator business, here's her step-by-step playbook: ✅ Build trust through consistency 👉🏼 Don't just post to post. Build trust, education, and community over time. Some clients follow you for years before purchasing. Long-term brand equity pays off ✅ Stop undercharging for one-offs 👉🏼 The real money is in packages. Bundle your content (reels, stories, newsletter mentions) into 3-6 month deals. Brands want long-term partners, not one-off posts. ✅ Niche down 👉🏼 Focus your efforts on a specific, underserved group you can truly relate to. Tara went all in on women 35+ when everyone told her to go broad. That specificity is exactly why her audience trusts her. ✅ Don't overbuild early 👉🏼 Tara's first paid program? A PDF she emailed to her list. That's it. She shipped before it was perfect. ✅ Scale by peeling away 👉🏼 To scale beyond yourself, slowly introduce a team and remove yourself as the sole face of the brand. Bring in coaches and build systems so your brand can run without you. ✅ Prioritize retention over acquisition 👉🏼 Keep clients longer instead of constantly chasing new ones. Real results take time, and people who stay become your best marketing. ✅ Treat whitelisting as premium 👉🏼 When a brand boosts your content, you're not just a creator. You're a spokesperson. Charge accordingly. We got into the real numbers: app subscriber margins, coaching economics, how she prices brand partnerships, and how she converts followers into paying customers. If you want to build a profitable creator business, this one's for you ☝🏻

AI search usage is up 70% year over year. Trust in it dropped 28 points in the same period. New Fractl and [Search Engine Land](https://www.linkedin.com/company/search-engine-land?trk=public%5Fprofile%5F%5Freactions-text) research surveyed over 1,000 consumers and 150 marketers. The findings are a wake-up call for anyone scaling content with AI. A few numbers worth sitting with: 39% of consumers now say heavy AI use in a brand's marketing reduces their trust, up from 20% last year. Over 80% want AI-generated content clearly labeled, across every format. Only 20% of brands actually disclose it. On the marketing side: AI now touches 53% of marketing work, up from 38% last year. But only 26% say AI made their work faster and better. Nearly half say it made their work faster but more generic. The most important line in the whole report: original research and proprietary data is the strategy that builds the strongest moat against AI commoditization, yet it is the least prioritized tactic at just 15%. Translation: everyone is racing to scale AI content. Almost nobody is investing in the thing AI cannot replicate. That gap is where the real opportunity sits right now. 87% of creators say AI has accelerated the growth of their business or audience. But 85% still say the final creative decision should always be theirs.[Adobe](https://www.linkedin.com/company/adobe?trk=public%5Fprofile%5F%5Freactions-text) just surveyed 16,000+ creators across 8 countries for its 2026 Creators' Toolkit Report. The findings line up exactly with what I keep saying about AI and content. 75% call creative AI integrated or essential to their workflow. 93% say it helps them produce content faster. But 57% say AI outputs still need moderate to extensive editing before they are ready to publish. Faster-to-draft is not the same as ready-to-publish. That gap is where the actual work still happens. The most interesting finding: as more people use AI, content volume goes up but standing out gets harder. 53% blame sheer content volume. 42% say AI-generated content is making unique voices harder to find. The creators winning anyway are not the ones avoiding AI. They are the ones who figured out how to make AI sound like them. 85% say their AI-assisted work still reflects their unique voice. Speed is now table stakes. Voice is the differentiator.
