Customer discovery is not the work of confirming what you believe. It is the work of finding the assumptions you did not know you were making. A founder I was coaching last week made an admission every seed-stage CEO should have to make out loud at least once. His team had built a product around a thesis: their industry needed legitimacy, and their software would deliver it. Three and a half years of work, a coherent narrative, a marketing strategy organized around that one story. Then they went and talked to customers. The customers told them nobody cared about legitimacy. What they cared about was insurance processing and scheduling, two features the founder had treated as table stakes. In 1959, the team that shipped COBOL was so used to being told it would fail that one of them had a tombstone made as a joke. Sixty years later, 200 billion lines of COBOL still ran the financial systems of the world. The assembly programmers who treated their craft as the job disappeared. The programmers who treated it as a layer they could climb above defined every decade that followed. Same pattern is repeating now. Eighteen months ago, "prompt engineer" was a job title. Today it is a paragraph in a README. We have moved from prompts, to agents, to skills, and skills are where it gets interesting. A skill is a documented, testable, reusable unit of know-how an AI can pick up, apply, and self-verify. That is not a coding pattern. That is a management pattern, and it maps cleanly onto Ken Blanchard's Situational Leadership. S1, you write the skill. S2, you coach the skill. S3, you let it verify its own output and escalate when it fails. S4, you delegate. The leader who can move an agent from S1 to S4 is doing the same job as the leader who can move a junior hire from S1 to S4\. Same muscle. Same human superpowers from Syntropy. More leverage every quarter. The new coder is not a typist. The new coder is a subject matter expert who wires agents and skills together, from the terminal, using tools like Claude Code, against a real problem. Nobody Complained About the Dark Until Someone Invented the Lamp Before 1979, accountants built financial models on paper. Change one assumption, recalculate every downstream figure by hand. The invisible problem is harder than the unsolved problem. And it takes five forms: 1\. Adaptation disguise. You restructured your workflow around the limitation so completely that the limitation vanished from view. 2\. Conceptual absence. The idea itself has not been invented yet. But because I've watched more than 25 companies spend their first 90 days running channels they didn't have the right to run yet, and the damage takes months to undo. I spent over a year embedded with a company doing $30M in ARR. They had a flagship logo, a freshly consolidated brand, and one goal: stop being the best-kept secret in the industry. They had four verticals. I said: pick one. The entire GTM motion requires you to know exactly who you're talking to before you spend a dollar reaching them. They said no. Funding pressure made everything urgent. The website said: we serve companies across the globe. The real market was the US and Canada. Within a couple of quarters, leads flooded in from India and Europe. Millions added to pipeline. Close to zero closed. The board saw a full pipeline. The CRO saw a ghost. That's what happens when you skip the sequence. The sequence trap is when a company starts building GTM motion before the foundational layer is locked. It doesn't look like a mistake. It looks like speed. But underneath, the ICP is blurry, the messaging is generic, and the demand you're capturing doesn't match the customers you can actually serve. Before you touch a single channel, lock six things: your market, your fit customers, your personas, your company's reality, your product's actual value, and the one problem you solve better than anyone else. Then ICP: companies, personas, locations specific enough to say no. Then messaging: pain, claim, gain. Then motion. Three layers. Applied in order. Layer 1 → Foundation: Calibration, not strategy. You can't aim at a target you haven't defined. Layer 2 → ICP + Messaging: Who exactly are you for? What exactly do you say to them? Layer 3 → Motion: Channels based on ICP buying behavior. Deploy across the funnel: create, capture, convert. Skip Layer 1, and you'll build a machine that produces the wrong output at scale. The company eventually narrowed. The ghost pipeline disappeared. The messaging started converting. They didn't need new channels. They needed the right sequence. Before I ever look at your CRM or your paid spend, I ask one question: Have you actually locked your ICP and messaging, or have you just assumed them? Most teams have assumed them. And most pipeline problems trace back to that single skip. The sequence is the strategy. P.S. LinkedIn compresses carousels on mobile, and I haven't figured out how to make them look readable yet.
